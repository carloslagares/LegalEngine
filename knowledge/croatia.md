# Croatia

COMPREHENSIVE VACATION RENTAL COMPLIANCE GUIDE FOR CROATIA

This guide covers the legal requirements for operating a short-term vacation rental
(private accommodation) in Croatia. It is written in English and structured as a
step-by-step compliance guide for property owners, including foreign nationals.

Croatia regulates short-term tourist rentals primarily through the Hospitality and
Catering Industry Act (Zakon o ugostiteljskoj djelatnosti, Official Gazette 85/15,
121/16, 99/18, 25/19, 98/19, 32/20, 42/20, 126/21, 59/23) and associated bylaws
on categorization, tourist tax, and guest registration. Private individuals who rent
accommodation to tourists are classified as "iznajmljivaci" (private renters) and
must follow a distinct registration and compliance path separate from commercial
hospitality businesses.

---

## 1. Legal Framework: Private Renter Status (Iznajmljivac)

In Croatia, a private individual who rents rooms, apartments, or holiday homes to
tourists is called an **iznajmljivac** (plural: iznajmljivaci). This status is
governed by the Hospitality and Catering Industry Act (Zakon o ugostiteljskoj
djelatnosti).

Key points about the legal framework:

- **Who can be an iznajmljivac:** Any Croatian citizen or EU/EEA citizen who owns
  or co-owns residential property can register as a private renter. Non-EU foreign
  nationals face additional requirements (see Section 11).
- **Types of accommodation allowed:** Rooms (sobe), apartments (apartmani),
  studio apartments (studio apartmani), and holiday homes (kuce za odmor).
- **Maximum capacity:** A private renter may offer up to **10 rooms** or **20 beds**
  in total across all units. If you exceed this threshold, you must register as a
  commercial hospitality business (obrt or company).
- **Entire property or rooms:** Unlike some jurisdictions, Croatia allows renting
  individual rooms as well as entire apartments or houses.
- **Duration:** There is no minimum or maximum stay length imposed by national law
  for private renters, though the accommodation is intended for short-term tourist
  stays.
- **Legal basis:** Zakon o ugostiteljskoj djelatnosti (Official Gazette 85/15 and
  amendments), Pravilnik o razvrstavanju i kategorizaciji objekata u kojima se
  pruzaju ugostiteljske usluge u domacinstvu (Ordinance on Classification and
  Categorization).

## 2. Step-by-Step Registration Process

### Step 1: Verify Property Eligibility

Before registering, confirm that:
- You own or co-own the property (or have written consent from all co-owners).
- The property is suitable for tourist accommodation (habitable, meets basic
  structural and safety standards).
- You do not exceed the 10-room / 20-bed limit for private renters.
- The property has a valid usage permit (uporabna dozvola) or equivalent
  documentation proving it is a legal residential structure.

### Step 2: Obtain Approval from the County Tourism Office

Registration is handled by the **county office** (zupanijski ured) responsible for
the area where the property is located, specifically the department for tourism or
economy (Ured drzavne uprave u zupaniji, odjel za turizam / gospodarstvo). In the
City of Zagreb, this is the City Office for Economy (Gradski ured za gospodarstvo).

**Documents to submit:**
1. **Application form** (Zahtjev za izdavanje rjesenja o odobrenju za pruzanje
   ugostiteljskih usluga u domacinstvu) -- available at the county office or online.
2. **Proof of ownership** -- land registry extract (izvadak iz zemljisne knjige)
   or equivalent ownership documentation.
3. **Co-owner consent** -- if the property has multiple owners, written consent
   from all co-owners is required.
4. **Proof of Croatian/EU citizenship** -- copy of personal ID (osobna iskaznica)
   or passport. For EU citizens, proof of EU nationality.
5. **Floor plan** (tlocrt) -- showing the layout and room dimensions.
6. **Proof of property legality** -- usage permit, building permit, or other
   documentation confirming the structure is legally built.
7. **Administrative fee** (upravna pristojba) -- a small fee for processing the
   application.

### Step 3: Categorization Inspection

After the application is submitted, the county office arranges a **categorization
inspection**. An authorized inspector visits the property to:
- Verify that it meets minimum standards for the requested category (star rating).
- Check room sizes, furnishings, equipment, and facilities.
- Assess safety and hygiene conditions.

Based on the inspection, the property receives:
- A **Decision on Categorization** (Rjesenje o kategorizaciji) specifying the
  type of accommodation (room, apartment, holiday home) and the star rating.
- A **categorization plaque** (plocica) that must be displayed at the entrance.

### Step 4: Register with the Tourist Board

After receiving the categorization decision, you must register with the **local
tourist board** (turisticka zajednica) in the municipality where the property is
located. This registration is necessary for:
- Tourist tax collection and reporting.
- Access to the eVisitor guest registration system.
- Inclusion in the local tourist board's records.

### Step 5: Register for the eVisitor System

You must register for access to the **eVisitor** system (see Section 3 below),
which is the mandatory electronic guest registration platform.

### Step 6: Register with the Tax Authority

You must register your rental activity with the **Croatian Tax Administration**
(Porezna uprava) to:
- Obtain a tax status (flat-rate or income-based taxation).
- Receive your tax identification and obligation details.

### Step 7: Obtain Liability Insurance

Take out a **liability insurance policy** covering guests and third parties
(see Section 10).

### Step 8: Begin Operations

Once you have:
- The categorization decision (rjesenje)
- Tourist board registration
- eVisitor access
- Tax registration
- Insurance

You may begin advertising and hosting guests. Your **registration number** must
appear on all advertisements and listings (Airbnb, Booking.com, etc.).

## 3. eVisitor System: Mandatory Guest Registration

The **eVisitor** system (www.evisitor.hr) is Croatia's centralized electronic
system for tourist check-in and check-out registration. It is operated by the
Croatian National Tourist Board (Hrvatska turisticka zajednica, HTZ) and is
**mandatory for all accommodation providers**, including private renters.

### Requirements:

- **Check-in registration:** Every guest must be registered in eVisitor **within
  24 hours of arrival** (previously the deadline was even shorter at the time of
  arrival for some categories, but the standard rule is within 24 hours).
- **Check-out registration:** The guest's departure must be recorded in eVisitor
  on the **day of departure**.
- **Data to be entered:** Guest's full name, date of birth, nationality, travel
  document type and number, date of arrival, date of departure, and type of
  accommodation.
- **Access:** Hosts receive login credentials from their local tourist board
  after registering their property.
- **Mobile access:** The system is accessible via web browser and has a mobile
  interface.

### Important Notes:

- Registration in eVisitor also serves as the mandatory reporting of guests to
  the **Ministry of the Interior** (MUP) under public safety regulations. Prior
  to eVisitor, hosts had to separately report guests to the police; this is now
  integrated.
- Failure to register guests in eVisitor is subject to fines (see Section 12).
- eVisitor automatically calculates the **tourist tax** (boravisna pristojba)
  owed based on check-in/check-out data.
- The system generates reports for the tourist board and tax authorities.
- All guests, including Croatian citizens staying in tourist accommodation, must
  be registered (though Croatian citizens are exempt from tourist tax in certain
  situations).

## 4. Categorization Requirements (Star Rating System)

Private accommodation in Croatia is categorized using a **star system** from
**2 stars to 4 stars** (privatni iznajmljivaci cannot receive 5 stars; that is
reserved for commercial hotel-type establishments). The categorization criteria
are set by the **Ordinance on Classification and Categorization of Accommodation
in Households** (Pravilnik o razvrstavanju i kategorizaciji objekata u kojima se
pruzaju ugostiteljske usluge u domacinstvu).

### Types of Private Accommodation:

| Type | Croatian Term | Description |
|------|--------------|-------------|
| Room | Soba | A furnished room with shared or private bathroom |
| Apartment | Apartman | Self-contained unit with kitchen/kitchenette, bathroom, sleeping area |
| Studio Apartment | Studio apartman | Smaller self-contained unit, open-plan |
| Holiday Home | Kuca za odmor | Entire house rented as a single unit |

### Star Categories and General Standards:

**2 Stars (two stars):**
- Basic, clean, functional accommodation.
- Minimum room size requirements met.
- Essential furniture and equipment provided.
- Adequate lighting and ventilation.

**3 Stars (three stars):**
- Good quality furnishings and equipment.
- Additional amenities (e.g., TV, better bathroom fittings).
- Higher standards of comfort and decor.
- More spacious rooms compared to 2-star.

**4 Stars (four stars):**
- High-quality furnishings and equipment.
- Superior comfort and aesthetic standards.
- Additional services or amenities (e.g., air conditioning, high-quality
  linens, modern appliances).
- Generous room sizes.

## 5. Technical and Quality Requirements

The Ordinance sets specific minimum requirements for each category. Below are
the key technical standards:

### Room Size Minimums:

| Room Type | 2 Stars | 3 Stars | 4 Stars |
|-----------|---------|---------|---------|
| Single bedroom | 6 m2 | 8 m2 | 10 m2 |
| Double bedroom | 8 m2 | 10 m2 | 12 m2 |
| Additional bed space | +2 m2 per person | +2 m2 per person | +3 m2 per person |

*Note: These are approximate minimums based on the categorization ordinance.
Kitchen areas, bathrooms, and common areas have separate size requirements.*

### Equipment and Furnishing Requirements:

**All Categories (minimum):**
- Bed with mattress, pillow, and bedding per guest.
- Wardrobe or closet with hangers.
- Mirror.
- Bedside table or shelf.
- Adequate lighting (general room light + reading light for higher categories).
- Curtains or blinds for darkness/privacy.
- Waste bin in each room.
- Fresh bed linens provided; changed at minimum every 7 days or between guests.
- Fresh towels provided; changed at minimum every 3 days or between guests.

**Bathroom Requirements:**
- Toilet, washbasin, and shower or bathtub.
- Hot and cold running water.
- Mirror, shelf or cabinet, towel rack.
- Soap or soap dispenser.
- Toilet paper.
- For 2-star rooms without en-suite: one shared bathroom per 5 guests maximum.
- For 3-star and above: en-suite (private) bathroom strongly expected or required.

**Kitchen/Kitchenette (for apartments and holiday homes):**
- Cooking surface (minimum 2 burners).
- Refrigerator.
- Sink with hot and cold water.
- Basic cooking utensils, pots, pans, plates, cutlery, glasses.
- Dining table and chairs.
- Dishwashing supplies.

**Additional Requirements for Higher Categories:**
- **3 stars:** TV, iron and ironing board (available on request), additional
  decorative elements, better quality furniture.
- **4 stars:** Air conditioning (in coastal areas, practically mandatory),
  satellite/cable TV, hairdryer, high-quality kitchen appliances (dishwasher,
  microwave recommended), internet/Wi-Fi access, superior bathroom fittings.

### Exterior and Common Areas:

- Property exterior must be well-maintained.
- Clear signage with categorization plaque at the entrance.
- Parking space availability is considered in categorization (not always
  mandatory but contributes to scoring).
- Outdoor areas (balconies, terraces, gardens) if advertised must be clean,
  furnished, and maintained.

## 6. Tourist Tax (Boravisna Pristojba)

The tourist tax in Croatia is regulated by the **Act on Tourist Tax**
(Zakon o boravisnoj pristojbi) and associated regulations. It is a per-person,
per-night charge that hosts must collect from guests and remit to the local
tourist board.

### Rate Structure:

Tourist tax rates vary by:
- **Tourist class of the municipality** (razred turistickog mjesta): Croatia
  classifies municipalities/cities into categories (A, B, C, D) based on their
  tourist significance. Class A locations (major coastal destinations like
  Dubrovnik, Split, Hvar) have the highest rates.
- **Season:** Rates differ between peak season (glavna sezona, typically
  June-September) and off-season (predsezona/posezona).

### Approximate Tourist Tax Rates (as of 2025):

| Municipality Class | Peak Season (per person/night) | Off-Season (per person/night) |
|-------------------|-------------------------------|------------------------------|
| A (top tourist destinations) | EUR 1.33 | EUR 0.66 |
| B | EUR 1.06 | EUR 0.53 |
| C | EUR 0.80 | EUR 0.40 |
| D | EUR 0.53 | EUR 0.27 |

*Note: Rates are set by government decree and may be adjusted. The rates above
are indicative based on the most recent published schedules. Always verify
current rates with your local tourist board.*

### Exemptions and Reductions:

- **Children under 12:** Exempt from tourist tax.
- **Children aged 12-17:** Pay 50% of the standard rate.
- **Organized groups of students:** Reduced rates may apply.
- **Persons with disabilities:** May qualify for exemptions.
- **Seasonal workers:** Exempt if registered as workers in the municipality.

### Collection and Payment:

- The host **collects** the tourist tax directly from guests.
- The host **remits** the collected tax to the local tourist board.
- Payment is typically due **by the 15th of the month** following the month
  in which the tax was collected.
- The eVisitor system tracks stays and calculates tax obligations automatically.
- The host receives a summary from eVisitor showing the total tax due.

## 7. Tax Obligations: Flat-Rate Taxation (Pausalni Porez)

Croatian citizens and EU residents who operate as private renters (iznajmljivaci)
can benefit from a simplified **flat-rate tax system** (pausalni porez), which is
one of the most attractive aspects of Croatian rental regulation.

### How Flat-Rate Taxation Works:

Instead of paying income tax on actual rental income (which would require
detailed bookkeeping), private renters pay a **fixed annual amount per bed**
(or per unit for apartments/holiday homes). This amount is set by the local
municipality/city council.

### Flat-Rate Tax Amounts:

The flat-rate tax per bed per year is determined by the **local government**
and varies based on the tourist classification of the area. The national
framework sets ranges, and municipalities choose within those ranges.

**Indicative annual flat-rate tax per bed (as of recent regulations):**

| Municipality Class | Annual Tax per Bed (EUR approximate) |
|-------------------|--------------------------------------|
| A (top destinations) | EUR 199 - 265 |
| B | EUR 133 - 199 |
| C | EUR 66 - 133 |
| D | EUR 33 - 66 |

*Note: These are approximate ranges. Each municipality sets its own rate within
the legally permitted range. Some municipalities also set per-unit rates for
apartments and holiday homes instead of per-bed rates.*

### Key Points about Flat-Rate Tax:

- The flat-rate tax covers **income tax and surtax** on rental income.
- It does **not** cover tourist tax (boravisna pristojba), which is separate.
- Payment is typically made in **three installments** during the year (by
  July 31, August 31, and September 30 of the current year, though the exact
  deadlines may vary).
- The flat-rate tax is due regardless of whether you actually rent the property
  or not during the year -- it is based on capacity, not occupancy.
- You do **not** need to keep detailed income/expense books under the flat-rate
  system, but you must keep records of guest stays (via eVisitor).
- If your total annual rental income exceeds **EUR 39,816.84** (300,000 HRK
  equivalent, though Croatia now uses EUR), you must switch to regular income
  tax accounting.

## 8. Sojourn Tax vs. Income Tax: Understanding the Difference

It is important to distinguish between the various taxes and charges:

### Tourist Tax (Boravisna Pristojba) -- "Sojourn Tax":
- **What:** A per-person, per-night fee collected from guests.
- **Who pays:** The guest pays; the host collects and remits.
- **Purpose:** Funds local tourist boards and tourism infrastructure.
- **Basis:** Number of guest-nights.
- **Recipient:** Local tourist board (turisticka zajednica).

### Flat-Rate Income Tax (Pausalni Porez):
- **What:** A fixed annual tax on rental income.
- **Who pays:** The host/owner.
- **Purpose:** Income tax on rental earnings.
- **Basis:** Number of beds/units (capacity), not actual income.
- **Recipient:** Tax Administration (Porezna uprava) / state budget.

### Regular Income Tax (Porez na dohodak):
- **What:** Standard income tax on net rental income (revenue minus allowable
  expenses).
- **Who pays:** The host/owner (if they opt out of or are ineligible for
  flat-rate taxation).
- **Rate:** Progressive rates of 20% (on income up to EUR 50,400 annually)
  and 30% (on income above EUR 50,400 annually), plus municipal surtax
  (prirez) which ranges from 0% to 18% depending on the municipality.
- **Basis:** Actual net rental income.
- **Recipient:** Tax Administration / state budget.

### VAT (PDV):
- Private renters operating under the flat-rate system are generally **not
  subject to VAT** as they fall below the VAT threshold (EUR 40,000 annual
  turnover for mandatory VAT registration as of recent rules).
- If operating as a commercial entity or exceeding the threshold, VAT at
  **13%** applies to accommodation services.

## 9. Insurance Requirements

### Mandatory Liability Insurance:

Croatian law requires private renters to have **liability insurance**
(osiguranje od odgovornosti) covering:
- Damage to guests caused by the accommodation or its deficiencies.
- Damage to guests' property.

### Recommended Coverage:

While the law mandates basic liability coverage, it is strongly recommended
to also carry:
- **General third-party liability insurance** -- covering injuries to guests
  on the property.
- **Property insurance** -- covering fire, water damage, natural disasters,
  and other risks to the building and contents.
- **Contents insurance** -- covering furnishings, equipment, and appliances.

### Minimum Coverage Amount:

The law does not specify a precise minimum coverage amount for private renters
in the same way as for hotels, but policies should provide adequate coverage.
Industry practice suggests a minimum of **EUR 50,000 - 100,000** in liability
coverage, though higher amounts are prudent, especially for larger or
higher-value properties.

### Where to Obtain Insurance:

Croatian insurance companies (e.g., Croatia osiguranje, Allianz Zagreb,
Generali, Euroherc) offer specific policies tailored to tourist rental
accommodation (polica osiguranja za iznajmljivace).

## 10. Safety Standards

### Fire Safety:

- Accommodation must comply with basic fire safety standards.
- Fire extinguisher should be available and accessible.
- Smoke detectors are recommended (and increasingly required in newer buildings).
- Emergency exit routes should be clear and, in multi-story buildings,
  evacuation plans should be visible.
- Gas installations (if any) must be regularly inspected and certified.

### Electrical Safety:

- Electrical installations must be safe and in working order.
- Properties should have an electrical installation inspection certificate
  (atest elektricnih instalacija) -- typically required every few years.

### Health and Hygiene:

- Drinking water must meet safety standards.
- If the property uses a private water supply (well), water quality testing
  may be required.
- Swimming pools (if any) must meet sanitary standards and be regularly tested.
- The property must be clean and free from health hazards (mold, pests, etc.).

### Building Safety:

- The property must be structurally sound.
- Balconies and terraces must have adequate railings.
- Stairs must be safe with handrails.
- Outdoor areas must be free from hazards.

### Pool Safety (if applicable):

- Pools must comply with sanitary inspection requirements.
- Regular water quality testing is mandatory.
- Fencing or barriers may be required depending on local regulations.
- Pool equipment must be maintained and safe.

## 11. Foreign Owner Requirements

### EU/EEA Citizens:

- EU/EEA citizens have the **same rights as Croatian citizens** to purchase
  property and register as private renters (iznajmljivaci).
- They must follow the same registration process described in Section 2.
- They need a Croatian **OIB** (Osobni identifikacijski broj -- Personal
  Identification Number) for tax and administrative purposes.

### Non-EU/EEA Citizens:

- Non-EU/EEA citizens **can purchase property** in Croatia, but only if there
  is a **reciprocity agreement** between Croatia and their home country. The
  Ministry of Justice maintains the list of countries with reciprocity.
- The property purchase must be approved by the **Ministry of Justice**
  (Ministarstvo pravosuđa), which can take several months.
- Once they own property, non-EU citizens can rent it out, but the process
  may involve additional administrative steps:
  - They may need to establish a **trade (obrt)** or **company (d.o.o.)** to
    operate the rental, rather than using the simpler iznajmljivac status,
    depending on their residency status.
  - If they have **temporary or permanent residency** in Croatia, they may
    qualify for iznajmljivac status similarly to citizens.
  - Without Croatian residency, operating through a registered business entity
    is typically required.
- **Tax obligations:** Non-residents are taxed on Croatian-source income. They
  must register with the Tax Administration and either use the flat-rate system
  (if eligible) or file regular income tax returns.
- **Tax treaties:** Croatia has double taxation treaties with many countries,
  which may affect how rental income is taxed.

### OIB Requirement:

All property owners, regardless of nationality, need a Croatian OIB number.
This can be obtained at any Tax Administration office with a valid passport
or ID.

## 12. Sanctions and Fines

Croatian authorities actively enforce vacation rental regulations. The
Hospitality and Catering Industry Act and related laws prescribe significant
fines for non-compliance:

### Operating Without Registration:

- Providing tourist accommodation without a valid categorization decision
  (rjesenje) is an **illegal activity**.
- Fines for operating without registration: **EUR 1,320 to EUR 13,270**
  for individuals (higher for legal entities).

### Failure to Register Guests (eVisitor):

- Not registering guests in the eVisitor system or failing to meet the
  24-hour deadline: fines of **EUR 260 to EUR 2,650** per violation.

### Failure to Collect/Remit Tourist Tax:

- Not collecting tourist tax from guests or not remitting it to the tourist
  board: fines of **EUR 660 to EUR 6,630**.

### Advertising Without Registration Number:

- Publishing rental advertisements without displaying the valid registration
  number: fines of **EUR 260 to EUR 2,650**.
- Platforms (Airbnb, Booking.com) are increasingly required to verify
  registration numbers.

### Categorization Violations:

- Operating in a category higher than assigned, or failing to maintain
  standards: fines and potential downgrade or revocation of categorization.
- Fines range from **EUR 260 to EUR 6,630** depending on the severity.

### Exceeding Capacity:

- Hosting more guests than the registered capacity: fines and potential
  revocation of the categorization decision.

### Tax Violations:

- Failure to pay flat-rate tax or income tax: penalties and interest as per
  the General Tax Act (Opci porezni zakon), including fines of up to
  **EUR 6,630** and potential criminal liability for tax evasion.

### Repeat Offenses:

- Repeat violations may result in **doubled fines** and/or **prohibition of
  activity** for a specified period.

## 13. Recent Regulatory Changes and Trends

### Euro Adoption (January 1, 2023):

- Croatia adopted the euro on January 1, 2023, replacing the Croatian kuna
  (HRK). All tax amounts, fines, and fees are now denominated in EUR.
- The conversion rate was fixed at 1 EUR = 7.53450 HRK.

### Increased Enforcement and Platform Cooperation:

- Croatian authorities have intensified enforcement against unregistered
  rentals, including cooperation with online platforms.
- Airbnb, Booking.com, and other platforms are increasingly required to
  share host data with Croatian authorities and to display registration
  numbers on listings.

### Digital Nomad Visa:

- Croatia introduced a Digital Nomad Visa/residence permit, which has
  increased demand for longer-term rental accommodation. However, this does
  not change the short-term rental regulatory framework.

### Amendments to the Hospitality Act (2023):

- The 2023 amendments (Official Gazette 59/23) introduced updates to the
  categorization process and strengthened enforcement provisions.
- Streamlined some administrative procedures for registration.
- Adjusted fine amounts to EUR-denominated values following euro adoption.

### Tourist Tax Adjustments:

- Tourist tax rates have been periodically adjusted. Following euro adoption,
  rates were converted and in some cases slightly rounded or adjusted.
- There is ongoing discussion about increasing tourist tax rates in the most
  popular destinations (Dubrovnik, Split, Hvar) to manage overtourism.

### Local Restrictions and Moratoriums:

- Some municipalities (notably Dubrovnik) have introduced or discussed
  **moratoriums** on new short-term rental registrations in certain zones,
  similar to measures seen in other European cities.
- The City of Dubrovnik has implemented restrictions on new permits in the
  Old Town area to preserve residential character.

### Schengen Accession (January 1, 2023):

- Croatia joined the Schengen Area on January 1, 2023. While this primarily
  affects border controls, it does not change the guest registration
  requirements. Hosts must still register all guests in eVisitor regardless
  of whether they come from a Schengen country.

### Upcoming Considerations:

- There is ongoing legislative discussion about further tightening
  regulations for short-term rentals, including potential caps on the number
  of rental properties per owner in certain areas.
- Possible introduction of mandatory energy performance certificates for
  tourist accommodation.
- Continued digitalization of registration and reporting processes.

Use this checklist to ensure full compliance:

- [ ] Property ownership or co-owner consent documented
- [ ] Application submitted to county tourism office
- [ ] Categorization inspection completed and decision received
- [ ] Categorization plaque displayed at property entrance
- [ ] Registered with local tourist board
- [ ] eVisitor system access obtained and operational
- [ ] Registered with Croatian Tax Administration
- [ ] Flat-rate tax or income tax status confirmed
- [ ] Liability insurance policy in place
- [ ] Fire extinguisher and safety equipment in place
- [ ] Registration number displayed on all online listings
- [ ] Guest registration process (eVisitor) in place for every booking
- [ ] Tourist tax collection and remittance process established
- [ ] Bed linens, towels, and required amenities stocked
- [ ] Emergency contact information available for guests

This guide is provided for informational purposes and reflects the regulatory
framework as understood based on Croatian legislation through 2025. Regulations
may change, and local municipalities may have additional requirements. Always

and tax administration before commencing rental operations. Professional legal
and tax advice is recommended, especially for foreign owners.
