# Italy

GUIDE FOR SHORT-TERM RENTAL COMPLIANCE IN ITALY

This comprehensive guide is designed to help property owners and managers navigate the
administrative and legal requirements for operating a short-term rental (locazione breve
or affitto turistico) in Italy. Italy has a multi-layered regulatory framework involving
national laws, regional legislation, and municipal ordinances, all of which must be
followed simultaneously.

Disclaimer: Italian short-term rental regulations are complex and evolve frequently.
This guide reflects the regulatory landscape as of early 2025, including the major
reforms introduced by Law No. 191/2023 (Budget Law 2024) and subsequent implementing
decrees. Always verify current requirements with local authorities, as regions and
municipalities may introduce additional rules.

## Regulatory Overview

Italy regulates short-term rentals through several layers of legislation:

**National Laws:**
- **Codice del Turismo** (D.Lgs. 79/2011): The consolidated code governing tourism
  activities in Italy, including accommodation structures.
- **Decreto-Legge 50/2017** (converted into Law 96/2017, Art. 4): Introduced the
  "locazione breve" regime for short-term rentals of up to 30 days, including the
  cedolare secca flat tax option and withholding obligations for platforms.
- **Law 191/2023** (Legge di Bilancio 2024, Art. 1, commi 71-75): Major reform
  introducing the CIN (Codice Identificativo Nazionale), new safety requirements,
  and the increase of cedolare secca to 26% for second and subsequent properties.
- **D.L. 145/2023** (Decreto Anticipi, converted into Law 191/2023): Contains
  provisions on the CIN database (BDSR - Banca Dati Strutture Ricettive).
- **Ministerial Decree of September 6, 2024**: Implementing decree establishing
  the operational procedures for the CIN and the BDSR national database.

**Regional Laws:** Each of Italy's 20 regions (plus 2 autonomous provinces) has its
own tourism legislation that may impose additional requirements regarding registration,
classification, maximum stay duration, and operational standards.

**Municipal Ordinances:** Cities can impose tourist taxes, zoning restrictions, and
additional operational requirements.

## Step 0: Your "Quick Folder" - Documents to Gather Before Starting

Before beginning any administrative process, collect the following:

- **Property deed or lease agreement** (contratto di locazione) authorizing tourist use.
- **Catasto (land registry) data**: Cadastral reference (foglio, particella, subalterno).
- **Floor plan and certificate of habitability** (certificato di agibilita, formerly
  certificato di abitabilita): Confirms the property meets habitability standards per
  D.P.R. 380/2001.
- **Owner's identity**: Codice fiscale and identity document of the property owner/manager.
- **Homeowners' association rules** (regolamento condominiale): Check for any prohibition
  on short-term rental activity.
- **APE (Attestato di Prestazione Energetica)**: Energy performance certificate, required
  for all rental contracts in Italy.
- **Property insurance documentation**.

## Step 1: Determine Your Rental Category

Italian law distinguishes between several types of short-term accommodation. Your
category determines which rules apply:

### A. Locazione Breve (Short-Term Lease)
- Governed by Art. 4 of D.L. 50/2017 (converted into Law 96/2017).
- Residential lease contracts of up to 30 days.
- Can only be entered into by **individuals** (not companies).
- May include ancillary services such as cleaning and linen provision.
- Does NOT include services typical of hotel operations (reception, meals, concierge).
  Adding such services reclassifies the activity as a "guest house" or similar
  accommodation structure subject to different regulations.
- Maximum of 4 properties can benefit from the locazione breve tax regime; beyond
  4 units, the activity is presumed to be entrepreneurial (Art. 1, comma 595,
  Law 178/2020).

### B. Struttura Ricettiva Extra-Alberghiera (Non-Hotel Accommodation)
- Includes B&Bs, affittacamere (guest houses), case vacanze (holiday homes), etc.
- Governed by regional tourism laws.
- Requires formal registration with the Region/Province as an accommodation structure.
- May provide additional services (breakfast for B&Bs, etc.).
- Subject to SCIA (Segnalazione Certificata di Inizio Attivita) filing.

### C. Casa Vacanze Gestita in Forma Imprenditoriale (Professionally Managed)
- If you manage more than 4 properties or provide hotel-like services, you must
  operate as a business (impresa).
- Requires a VAT number (Partita IVA), business registration, and compliance with
  regional regulations for accommodation structures.

## Step 2: CIN - Codice Identificativo Nazionale (National Identification Code)

**Legal Basis:** Art. 13-ter of D.L. 145/2023, as modified by Law 191/2023 (Budget Law
2024); Ministerial Decree of September 6, 2024.

The CIN is the cornerstone of Italy's new national regulatory framework for short-term
rentals. It became **mandatory from January 2, 2025** (following the transitional period
that began September 2, 2024).

### What is the CIN?
- A unique alphanumeric national identification code assigned to every short-term
  rental property and tourist accommodation structure in Italy.
- It replaces or complements pre-existing regional codes (CIR - Codice Identificativo
  Regionale) that were previously used in some regions.
- It is managed through the **BDSR (Banca Dati delle Strutture Ricettive)**, the
  national database operated by the Ministry of Tourism.

### Who Must Obtain a CIN?
- All owners/managers of:
  - Short-term rental properties (locazioni brevi).
  - Non-hotel accommodation structures (B&B, affittacamere, case vacanze, etc.).
  - Hotel and similar accommodation facilities.
- Both individuals and businesses must obtain the CIN.

### How to Obtain the CIN
1. **Access the BDSR portal** at https://bdsr.ministeroturismo.gov.it via SPID
   (Sistema Pubblico di Identita Digitale) or CIE (Carta d'Identita Elettronica).
2. **Submit a self-declaration** (dichiarazione sostitutiva) attesting:
   - Cadastral data of the property.
   - Compliance with safety requirements (fire extinguishers, gas/CO detectors).
   - Compliance with habitability/building standards.
3. **If you already have a CIR** (regional code), link it to the CIN application.
   The CIN incorporates the regional code into a national format.
4. **The CIN is assigned automatically** once the self-declaration is validated.
   Format: IT + Region Code + Province Code + Alphanumeric string
   (e.g., IT09LO12345A).

### CIN Display Obligations
- The CIN must be displayed:
  - **Outside the property**: On a visible sign/plaque near the entrance.
  - **On all advertisements and listings**: Every online and offline advertisement,
    including listings on platforms (Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo, etc.), must show
    the CIN.
- Platforms are required to verify the CIN and may not publish listings without it.

### Fines for CIN Non-Compliance
- **Operating without a CIN**: Fine from EUR 800 to EUR 8,000.
- **Failure to display the CIN** on the property or advertisements: Fine from
  EUR 500 to EUR 5,000.
- Platforms that allow listings without a CIN face fines from EUR 500 to EUR 5,000
  per listing.

## Step 3: Regional Registration (CIR or SCIA)

Before or alongside obtaining the CIN, most regions require a separate registration:

### Regional Code (CIR - Codice Identificativo Regionale)
- Many regions already had their own registration systems before the CIN was introduced.
- The CIR is typically obtained through the regional tourism portal or SUAP (Sportello
  Unico Attivita Produttive) of the municipality.
- Examples of regional systems:
  - **Lombardy**: Registration via the "Turismo5" platform.
  - **Tuscany**: Registration via the "Turismo" section of the Regione Toscana portal.
  - **Lazio**: Registration via "SUAR" (Sportello Unico delle Attivita Ricettive).
  - **Veneto**: Registration via "GesTur" portal.
  - **Sicily**: Registration via the "Dipartimento Turismo" portal.
  - **Sardinia**: Registration via "SIRED" (Sistema Informativo Regionale).

### SCIA (Segnalazione Certificata di Inizio Attivita)
- Required for non-hotel accommodation structures (B&Bs, affittacamere, case
  vacanze operated professionally).
- Filed with the SUAP of the municipality where the property is located.
- Must include: property data, floor plan, habitability certificate, proof of
  safety compliance, insurance details.
- For simple locazioni brevi (short-term leases by individuals), a SCIA is
  typically NOT required, but a "comunicazione" (notification) to the municipality
  or region may be.

## Step 4: Alloggiati Web - Police Registration of Guests

**Legal Basis:** Art. 109 of the TULPS (Testo Unico delle Leggi di Pubblica Sicurezza,
R.D. 773/1931); D.M. of November 7, 2017.

### Obligation
- **All** accommodation providers, including private owners offering short-term
  rentals (locazioni brevi), must register guest identity data with the police.
- This applies to Italian citizens, EU citizens, and non-EU citizens.

### How It Works
1. **Register with the local Questura** (police headquarters) to obtain access
   credentials for the Alloggiati Web system (https://alloggiatiweb.poliziadistato.it).
2. **Collect guest identity data** upon check-in:
   - Full name, date and place of birth, citizenship, residence.
   - Identity document type, number, and issuing authority.
   - For non-EU guests: passport number, visa details if applicable.
   - For groups/families: the head of the group provides documents for all members.
3. **Transmit data electronically** via Alloggiati Web within **24 hours of arrival**
   (or within 6 hours for stays of less than 24 hours).
4. **For single-night stays**: Data must be communicated immediately upon check-in.

### Important Notes
- As of 2025, Italy has been working on transitioning to the **SES (Schedina
  Elettronica di Sorveglianza)** system, an updated version of Alloggiati Web
  that collects additional data points. Check with your local Questura for the
  most current system in use.
- Failure to register guests carries criminal penalties under Art. 17 TULPS:
  arrest for up to 3 months or a fine up to EUR 206. Repeat offenses can lead
  to suspension of the rental activity.

## Step 5: ISTAT Statistical Reporting

**Legal Basis:** D.Lgs. 322/1989 (National Statistics System); regional implementing
regulations.

### Obligation
- All accommodation providers must report occupancy data to ISTAT (Istituto
  Nazionale di Statistica) through the municipal or regional tourism office.
- This is a **statistical** obligation, separate from the police registration.

### Data to Report (Monthly or Per Stay)
- Number of guests (arrivals and departures).
- Nationality of guests.
- Number of overnight stays (presenze).
- Dates of arrival and departure.

### How to Report
- The reporting method varies by region and municipality:
  - Some use dedicated portals (e.g., "Radar" in Tuscany, "Ross1000" in
    Emilia-Romagna, "Turismo5" in Lombardy).
  - Others accept data via the municipal tourism office or PEC (certified email).
- Deadlines are typically within the first 5-10 days of the following month,
  though some municipalities require real-time reporting.

### Fines
- Failure to report: Administrative fines vary by region, typically EUR 500 to
  EUR 2,500 per violation.

## Step 6: Tourist Tax (Imposta di Soggiorno)

**Legal Basis:** D.Lgs. 23/2011, Art. 4; subsequent municipal deliberations.

### Overview
- The tourist tax is a municipal tax levied on overnight stays in accommodation
  structures, including short-term rentals.
- Not all municipalities impose it; it is most common in tourist-heavy cities
  and towns.
- The host is responsible for **collecting** the tax from guests and **remitting**
  it to the municipality.

### Role of the Host
- As of D.L. 50/2017 and subsequent clarifications, hosts acting as short-term
  rental operators are considered **responsible for tax collection** (responsabili
  d'imposta).
- Hosts must:
  1. Inform guests about the tax.
  2. Collect the tax upon check-in or checkout.
  3. Remit the tax to the municipality per the local schedule (monthly, quarterly,
     or annually depending on the municipality).
  4. File a periodic declaration (dichiarazione) with the municipality.

### Tax Rates by Major City (approximate, subject to annual updates)

| City | Rate per Person per Night | Maximum Nights Taxed | Notes |
|------|--------------------------|---------------------|-------|
| **Rome** | EUR 3.50 - EUR 7.00 | 10 nights | Varies by property type and zone; higher rates for properties in the historic center. Budget Law 2025 authorized Rome to increase rates for the Jubilee Year 2025. |
| **Milan** | EUR 2.00 - EUR 5.00 | No cap | Varies by accommodation category. |
| **Florence** | EUR 3.00 - EUR 5.50 | 7 consecutive nights | Among the highest in Italy. Differentiated by accommodation type. |
| **Venice** | EUR 1.00 - EUR 5.00 | 5 nights | Venice also charges a separate "Contributo di Accesso" (day-tripper entry fee) since 2024, but this is separate from the tourist tax. |
| **Naples** | EUR 1.00 - EUR 5.00 | 14 nights | |
| **Bologna** | EUR 2.50 - EUR 5.00 | 10 nights | |
| **Turin** | EUR 1.30 - EUR 5.00 | Varies | |
| **Palermo** | EUR 0.50 - EUR 2.00 | 4 nights | |

### Exemptions (Common, but Vary by Municipality)
- Children under a certain age (typically 10-14 years).
- Residents of the municipality.
- Patients in medical treatment.
- Law enforcement and military on duty.
- Guests staying for extended periods beyond the taxable maximum.

### Fines
- Failure to collect or remit the tourist tax: Fines of 100% to 200% of the
  unpaid amount, plus interest. In some cases, criminal liability for
  misappropriation (peculato) under Art. 314 of the Penal Code may apply,
  as the host acts as a tax collection agent.

## Step 7: Cedolare Secca - Flat Tax for Short-Term Rentals

**Legal Basis:** D.Lgs. 23/2011, Art. 3; D.L. 50/2017, Art. 4; Law 213/2023
(Budget Law 2024), Art. 1, comma 63.

### What is Cedolare Secca?
- A flat-rate substitute tax (imposta sostitutiva) that replaces the ordinary
  progressive IRPEF income tax, regional/municipal surcharges, and registration
  tax on rental income.
- Available only to **individual persons** (persone fisiche) who do not operate
  in a business capacity. Companies and VAT-registered entities cannot use it.

### Tax Rates (from January 1, 2024)
- **21%** on rental income from the **first** short-term rental property.
- **26%** on rental income from the **second, third, and fourth** properties
  rented on a short-term basis (up to 30 days).
- The taxpayer can designate which property benefits from the 21% rate each year.
- These rates apply specifically to locazioni brevi (up to 30 days). For longer
  rentals (over 30 days) with the cedolare secca option, the standard rate of
  21% applies (or 10% for "canone concordato" contracts).

### Maximum Number of Properties
- As established by Art. 1, comma 595 of Law 178/2020, if a person rents more
  than **4 properties** for short-term stays, the activity is presumed to be
  entrepreneurial, regardless of location. In this case:
  - Cedolare secca is no longer available.
  - A VAT number (Partita IVA) is required.
  - Business income rules apply (reddito d'impresa).

### Platform Withholding
- Since January 1, 2024, platforms (Airbnb, Booking.com, etc.) acting as
  intermediaries must apply a **21% withholding tax** (ritenuta) on payments
  made to hosts. This withholding acts as:
  - A **definitive tax** (ritenuta a titolo d'imposta) if the host opts for
    cedolare secca.
  - An **advance payment** (ritenuta a titolo d'acconto) if the host is
    subject to ordinary IRPEF taxation.
- The host must still declare the income in their annual tax return (Modello
  Redditi or 730) and reconcile any difference.

### How to Opt for Cedolare Secca
- For contracts exceeding 30 days: The option is exercised at the time of
  contract registration with the Agenzia delle Entrate.
- For locazioni brevi (up to 30 days): The option is exercised in the annual
  tax return by indicating the income in the relevant section (Quadro B or
  Quadro LC of the tax return).

## Step 8: Safety Requirements

**Legal Basis:** Art. 1, commi 71-75 of Law 191/2023 (Budget Law 2024);
D.M. September 6, 2024.

The Budget Law 2024 introduced mandatory safety requirements for all short-term
rental properties and tourist accommodation structures. Compliance is self-declared
when applying for the CIN and may be verified through inspections.

### Mandatory Safety Equipment
All short-term rental properties must be equipped with:

#### 1. Fire Extinguishers
- At least one portable fire extinguisher must be installed in the property.
- Must comply with UNI EN 3 standards.
- Must be regularly maintained and inspected (every 6 months) per D.M.
  September 1, 2021 (maintenance of fire protection equipment).
- Must be easily accessible and visible, with appropriate signage.

#### 2. Smoke and Gas Detectors
- **Smoke detectors** (rilevatori di fumo): Recommended and in many cases
  required by regional fire safety norms for residential properties.
- **Gas detectors** (rilevatori di gas): Required in properties with gas
  appliances (stoves, water heaters, heating systems running on gas).
  Must be installed near gas appliances per UNI EN 50194 standards.

#### 3. Carbon Monoxide (CO) Detectors
- **Mandatory** in all properties with combustion appliances (gas boilers,
  fireplaces, wood-burning stoves, gas water heaters).
- Must comply with UNI EN 50291 standards.
- Must be installed in rooms containing or adjacent to combustion appliances.

### Additional Safety Considerations
- **Electrical system**: Must comply with D.M. 37/2008 (formerly Law 46/1990).
  A "dichiarazione di conformita" (declaration of conformity) from a qualified
  electrician is advisable.
- **Gas system**: If present, must comply with D.M. 37/2008 and UNI 7129 standards.
  Periodic inspection and certification recommended.
- **Emergency signage**: While not strictly mandatory for residential properties,
  placing evacuation plans and emergency contact information is best practice
  and required by some regional laws.
- **First aid kit**: Not nationally mandated but recommended.
- **Swimming pools**: If the property has a pool, specific safety regulations
  apply (fencing, depth markings, rescue equipment) per regional health authority
  (ASL) requirements.

### Fines for Safety Non-Compliance
- Providing a false self-declaration regarding safety equipment when applying
  for the CIN can result in fines from EUR 600 to EUR 6,000.
- Failure to maintain required equipment may result in additional penalties
  under fire safety regulations.

## Step 9: Insurance Requirements

### Civil Liability Insurance (Responsabilita Civile)
- While not nationally mandated for all locazioni brevi by a single statute,
  **civil liability insurance** is:
  - **Required by many regional laws** for registered accommodation structures
    (B&B, affittacamere, case vacanze).
  - **Strongly recommended** for all short-term rental operators.
  - **Required by some platforms** (Airbnb provides its own "AirCover" program,
    but this should not replace dedicated insurance).

### What Insurance Should Cover
- **Guest injuries**: Bodily harm suffered by guests on the property.
- **Property damage to third parties**: Damage caused to neighbors or common
  areas of the building.
- **Guest property damage**: Damage to guests' personal belongings.
- **Structural defects**: Liability arising from building defects.

### Recommended Coverage
- Minimum recommended coverage: EUR 500,000 to EUR 1,000,000 per claim.
- Consider also:
  - **Property insurance** (assicurazione dell'immobile) covering fire, theft,
    water damage, and natural disasters.
  - **Contents insurance** for furniture and equipment.
  - **Loss of income insurance** covering periods when the property cannot
    be rented due to damage.

## Step 10: Electronic Invoicing (Fatturazione Elettronica)

**Legal Basis:** D.Lgs. 127/2015; Provvedimento Agenzia delle Entrate of
April 30, 2018.

### When Electronic Invoicing Applies
- Electronic invoicing (fatturazione elettronica) through the SDI (Sistema
  di Interscambio) is **mandatory for all VAT-registered entities** in Italy.
- For **individual hosts** operating under the locazione breve regime (up
  to 30 days) **without a VAT number**, electronic invoicing is NOT required.
  These hosts issue a simple receipt (ricevuta) or no document at all, as the
  income is declared in the annual tax return.
- For **property managers and agencies** operating with a VAT number, electronic
  invoicing is mandatory for all services rendered.

### Key Rules for VAT-Registered Operators
- All invoices must be issued in XML format and transmitted through the SDI.
- Invoice must include: CIN of the property, description of the service,
  dates of stay, applicable VAT rate.
- Invoices must be issued within 12 days of the taxable event.
- Use certified software or the free Agenzia delle Entrate portal for
  electronic invoicing.

### For Platforms
- Platforms operating in Italy (Airbnb, Booking.com) issue their invoices
  for platform fees in electronic format.
- The 21% withholding tax applied by platforms is documented through the
  CU (Certificazione Unica) issued annually by the platform to the host
  and to the Agenzia delle Entrate.

## Step 11: Rental Contract Requirements

### Contracts Up to 30 Days (Locazione Breve)
- No mandatory written contract is legally required, but having one is
  **strongly recommended**.
- No registration with the Agenzia delle Entrate is required.
- No stamp duty (imposta di bollo) or registration tax is due.
- The contract should specify: parties, property address, dates, price,
  house rules, cancellation policy.

### Contracts Over 30 Days
- A written contract is **mandatory**.
- Must be registered with the Agenzia delle Entrate within 30 days of
  signing.
- Registration tax applies (2% of annual rent, or waived if cedolare secca
  is chosen).
- Stamp duty: EUR 16 for every 4 pages or 100 lines.

## Step 13: Regional Variations - Key Differences

Each Italian region has its own tourism law. Below are highlights of notable
regional rules:

### Lombardy (Milan, Lake Como, Bergamo)
- Regional Law 27/2015 and subsequent amendments.
- Registration via "Turismo5" portal.
- Short-term rental units classified as "CAV" (Case e Appartamenti per Vacanze)
  or "Locazioni Turistiche."
- Milan imposes strict rules on maximum annual rental days in certain zones.

### Lazio (Rome)
- Regional Law 13/2007 and D.G.R. (Regional Government Decisions).
- Registration via SUAR system.
- Rome has among the highest tourist taxes in Italy, further increased for the
  2025 Jubilee Year.
- Specific rules for properties in the historic center (Centro Storico).

### Tuscany (Florence, Siena, Pisa)
- Regional Law 42/2000 (modified by L.R. 86/2016).
- Registration via regional tourism portal.
- Florence has implemented restrictions on new short-term rental permits in the
  UNESCO historic center (from June 2023), banning new vacation rental licenses
  in the centro storico.
- Mandatory ISTAT reporting via "Radar" system.

### Veneto (Venice, Verona, Padua)
- Regional Law 11/2013.
- Registration via "GesTur" portal.
- Venice has some of the strictest regulations, including caps on new rental
  permits in certain areas and requirements for on-site key handover (no key
  boxes in some zones per municipal ordinance).
- Venice's "Contributo di Accesso" (access fee for day-trippers, EUR 5) is
  separate from the tourist tax.

### Campania (Naples, Amalfi Coast, Capri)
- Regional Law 5/2001 and subsequent modifications.
- Registration through regional portal or SUAP.
- The Amalfi Coast municipalities may have additional specific requirements.

### Sicily
- Regional Law 6/2001.
- Registration via the Department of Tourism portal.
- Tax benefits may apply for properties in certain under-tourism areas.

### Sardinia
- Regional Law 16/2017.
- Registration via "SIRED" system.
- Strict environmental and building regulations in coastal areas.

### Puglia (Bari, Lecce, Salento)
- Regional Law 17/2015.
- Registration via regional tourism portal.
- Growing regulatory attention due to tourism boom in the Salento area.

### Emilia-Romagna (Bologna, Rimini, Parma)
- Regional Law 16/2004.
- ISTAT reporting via "Ross1000" system.
- Bologna has introduced restrictions and higher tourist tax rates.

### South Tyrol / Alto Adige (Bolzano)
- Autonomous Province with its own tourism law (L.P. 9/2018).
- Very strict regulations: limits on the number of beds, mandatory registration,
  high standards for property quality.
- Bilingual requirements (German/Italian) for all guest documentation.

### Trentino (Trento)
- Autonomous Province with specific regulations (L.P. 7/2002).
- Mandatory registration and classification.
- Tourism is highly regulated with quality standards.

## Step 14: Step-by-Step Compliance Checklist

Use this checklist to ensure full compliance before listing your property:

- [ ] **1. Verify property eligibility**: Confirm habitability certificate (agibilita),
  check condominium rules, verify zoning compatibility.
- [ ] **2. Determine rental category**: Locazione breve, B&B, affittacamere, or
  casa vacanze. This determines your regulatory path.
- [ ] **3. Regional registration**: Register with your region's tourism portal to
  obtain the CIR (regional code) where required.
- [ ] **4. Obtain the CIN**: Register on the BDSR national database
  (bdsr.ministeroturismo.gov.it) to obtain your Codice Identificativo Nazionale.
- [ ] **5. Install safety equipment**: Fire extinguisher(s), gas detectors (if gas
  appliances present), CO detectors (if combustion appliances present).
- [ ] **6. Display the CIN**: Place the CIN on a visible sign outside the property
  and include it in all listings and advertisements.
- [ ] **7. Register with the Questura**: Obtain Alloggiati Web credentials from
  your local police headquarters.
- [ ] **8. Set up ISTAT reporting**: Register with the municipal or regional
  statistical reporting system.
- [ ] **9. Check tourist tax obligations**: Verify if your municipality charges
  an imposta di soggiorno and register as a collector.
- [ ] **10. Arrange insurance**: Obtain civil liability insurance (mandatory in
  many regions, strongly recommended everywhere).
- [ ] **11. Choose your tax regime**: Decide between cedolare secca (21%/26%)
  or ordinary IRPEF taxation. Consult a commercialista (tax advisor).
- [ ] **12. Prepare guest documentation**: House rules, emergency information,
  check-in/checkout procedures, Wi-Fi information, local emergency numbers.
- [ ] **13. Ensure ongoing compliance**: Report guests within 24 hours, collect
  and remit tourist tax, file ISTAT reports, maintain safety equipment, file
  annual tax returns.
