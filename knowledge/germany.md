# Germany

VACATION RENTAL COMPLIANCE GUIDE FOR GERMANY
This guide provides a step-by-step overview of the administrative, legal, and tax requirements
for operating a short-term vacation rental (Ferienwohnung) in Germany. Because Germany is a
federal republic, rules vary significantly between states (Bundeslaender) and cities. This guide
covers the federal framework and then the most important city-specific regulations.

Important Note: Germany does not have a single national short-term rental law. Instead,
individual states and municipalities enact their own "misuse prohibition laws"
(Zweckentfremdungsverbote). Always verify current local rules with the relevant city authority
(Bezirksamt or Stadtverwaltung) before listing a property.

---

## Step 1: Understand the Legal Framework

### Federal Level
Germany has no unified federal law specifically governing short-term vacation rentals. The
relevant legal landscape consists of:

- **Baunutzungsverordnung (BauNVO)** - Federal Building Use Ordinance: Defines permitted
  uses in residential, mixed-use, and commercial zones. In purely residential zones
  (Reines Wohngebiet), short-term letting may be restricted or prohibited.
- **Buergerliches Gesetzbuch (BGB)** - Civil Code: Governs landlord-tenant relationships.
  Tenants must obtain explicit landlord consent to sublet, including short-term.
- **Bundesmeldegesetz (BMG)** - Federal Registration Act: Requires all accommodation
  providers to register guests using a registration form (Meldeschein).
- **Gewerbeordnung (GewO)** - Trade Regulation Act: If rental activity is conducted
  commercially (gewerblich), a trade registration (Gewerbeanmeldung) may be required.

### State and Municipal Level - Zweckentfremdungsverbot
Many German states have enacted enabling legislation allowing municipalities to pass
"misuse prohibition" ordinances (Zweckentfremdungsverbote). These laws aim to protect
residential housing stock by restricting or prohibiting the conversion of residential space
to other uses, including short-term vacation rental.

Cities that have enacted Zweckentfremdungsverbote include:
- **Berlin** (Zweckentfremdungsverbot-Gesetz, ZwVbG)
- **Munich** (Zweckentfremdungssatzung)
- **Hamburg** (Wohnraumschutzgesetz, HmbWoSchG)
- **Cologne** (Wohnraumschutzsatzung)
- **Duesseldorf** (Zweckentfremdungssatzung)
- **Stuttgart** (Zweckentfremdungsverbotssatzung)
- **Frankfurt am Main** (Zweckentfremdungssatzung)
- **Freiburg**, **Konstanz**, **Muenster**, and many others

Not all German cities have such restrictions. In smaller towns and rural areas, short-term
rental is often unrestricted beyond standard trade and tax law.

## Step 2: Determine if Your Property Requires a Permit

### When a Permit Is Required
In cities with a Zweckentfremdungsverbot, renting out residential space on a short-term basis
(typically fewer than 2-3 months per stay) is considered "misuse" (Zweckentfremdung) of
housing and requires prior approval. This applies when:

- You rent out an **entire apartment** that would otherwise be available on the residential
  rental market.
- You rent **more than 50% of your primary residence** for extended periods.
- In some cities, even renting out individual rooms may require registration or approval,
  depending on local rules.

### When a Permit Is Generally NOT Required
- Renting a **room within your own primary residence** (your main home) while you continue
  to live there is often exempt or subject to simplified rules. However, many cities still
  require notification or registration.
- Properties in areas **without a Zweckentfremdungsverbot** in effect.
- Properties classified as **commercial/holiday accommodation** from the outset (e.g.,
  purpose-built holiday apartments in tourist areas with appropriate zoning).

### How to Apply
Each city has its own application process. Typically, you must submit:
1. Proof of property ownership or landlord consent.
2. Floor plans showing the property layout.
3. Description of the intended use (frequency, duration, platform).
4. In some cities, proof that the property is your primary residence.

Processing times vary from a few weeks to several months.

## Step 3: City-Specific Rules

### Berlin
Berlin has Germany's strictest and most well-known vacation rental regulations.

**Governing Law:** Zweckentfremdungsverbot-Gesetz (ZwVbG), in effect since May 1, 2014,
significantly tightened in 2018 and amended again in 2022.

**Key Rules:**
- **Registration Number Required:** All short-term rental hosts must obtain a
  **Wohnraumschutznummer** (housing protection number, often called a registration number)
  from the district office (Bezirksamt) before advertising the property. This number must
  be displayed in **every listing and advertisement**.
- **Primary Residence Rule:** You may rent out your **primary residence** (Hauptwohnung)
  for up to **90 days per calendar year** without special approval, provided you have a
  valid registration number.
- **Secondary Residences / Entire Apartments:** Renting out a secondary residence or an
  apartment that is not your primary residence requires an **exceptional approval**
  (Ausnahmegenehmigung) from the Bezirksamt. These approvals are very difficult to obtain
  and are typically denied in areas with tight housing markets.
- **Room Rentals:** Renting out up to **49% of your primary residence** (e.g., a single
  room) is generally permitted without a day limit, but you still need the registration
  number and must continue living in the apartment.
- **Registration Number Display:** The Wohnraumschutznummer must appear on all platforms,
  websites, and advertisements. Failure to display it is an independent violation.

**Application Process:**
- Apply at your local Bezirksamt (district office).
- Required documents: ID, proof of primary residence registration (Meldebestaetigung),
  property ownership documents or landlord consent, floor plan.
- Processing time: Typically 4-8 weeks.

**Fines:**
- Violations can result in fines of up to **EUR 500,000**.
- Fines apply for: renting without a registration number, exceeding the 90-day limit,
  failing to display the registration number, providing false information.
- Berlin authorities actively enforce these rules using automated monitoring of platforms.

### Munich
**Governing Law:** Zweckentfremdungssatzung der Landeshauptstadt Muenchen, based on the
Bavarian Zweckentfremdungsgesetz (ZwEWG).

**Key Rules:**
- Short-term rental of residential space requires a **permit** (Genehmigung) from the
  Department of Housing (Amt fuer Wohnen und Migration).
- **Primary Residence Exception:** Renting out your primary residence is permitted for up
  to **8 weeks (56 days) per calendar year** without a permit. Beyond 8 weeks, a permit
  is required.
- **Secondary Residences:** Any short-term rental of a secondary residence requires a permit.
- **Registration Requirement:** Hosts must register with the city and obtain a registration
  number that must be included in all advertisements.
- **Compensatory Measures:** Permit holders may be required to provide compensatory housing
  or pay a compensatory fee.

**Fines:**
- Up to **EUR 500,000** for violations.

### Hamburg
**Governing Law:** Hamburgisches Wohnraumschutzgesetz (HmbWoSchG).

**Key Rules:**
- Short-term rental of residential space (Wohnraum) requires a **permit** from the relevant
  district office (Bezirksamt).
- **Primary Residence Exception:** Hosts may rent out their primary residence short-term
  without a permit, but must **register** the activity with the district office and obtain
  a **Wohnraumschutznummer** (registration number).
- The registration number must be displayed in all listings.
- There is no fixed day-limit for primary residences like Berlin's 90-day rule, but the
  rental must remain occasional and the property must remain the host's primary home.
- **Secondary Residences / Investment Properties:** Require a full permit. Permits may
  be denied if the property is needed for the residential market.

**Fines:**
- Up to **EUR 500,000** for violations.

### Duesseldorf
**Governing Law:** Zweckentfremdungssatzung of the City of Duesseldorf, based on the
North Rhine-Westphalia Wohnraumstaerkungsgesetz.

**Key Rules:**
- Short-term rental of residential space requires **approval** from the city.
- **Primary Residence Exception:** Renting out your primary residence for short periods
  may be permitted without a full permit, but registration is required.
- A **registration number** must be obtained and displayed in listings.
- The city monitors compliance actively and cooperates with platforms.

**Fines:**
- Up to **EUR 500,000** for violations under the NRW state law framework.

### Cologne
**Governing Law:** Wohnraumschutzsatzung of the City of Cologne (Koeln), also based on
the NRW Wohnraumstaerkungsgesetz.

**Key Rules:**
- The entire city of Cologne is covered by the housing protection ordinance.
- **Permit Required:** Short-term rental of residential space requires prior approval.
- **Registration Number:** Hosts must register and display a registration number.
- **Primary Residence:** Renting out rooms in your primary residence while you continue
  to live there is subject to simplified rules, but still requires notification.
- Cologne has been increasingly active in enforcement since 2020.

**Fines:**
- Up to **EUR 500,000** for violations.

### Other Notable Cities
- **Stuttgart:** Zweckentfremdungsverbotssatzung in effect. Permit required for short-term
  rental. Registration number mandatory. Fines up to EUR 100,000.
- **Frankfurt am Main:** Zweckentfremdungssatzung in effect. Permit required. Strict
  enforcement especially in the city center. Fines up to EUR 500,000 under Hessian law.
- **Freiburg:** Zweckentfremdungssatzung in effect. Registration and permit required.

## Step 4: Guest Registration (Meldeschein)

### Federal Requirement
Under the **Bundesmeldegesetz (BMG), Sections 29-30**, all accommodation providers in
Germany are **legally required** to have guests fill out a registration form (Meldeschein)
upon arrival.

**What Must Be Recorded:**
- Full name of each guest (including maiden name if applicable).
- Date of birth.
- Home address (permanent residence).
- Nationality.
- Date of arrival and planned date of departure.
- Number of accompanying persons (names of adults, number of children).
- For **foreign guests**: Passport or ID number, issuing authority, date of issue.

**Requirements:**
- The guest must **personally sign** the Meldeschein.
- The host must **verify identity** using an official identification document (passport
  or national ID card).
- The Meldeschein must be **kept for one year** after the guest's departure and made
  available to authorities upon request.
- **Digital registration** is permitted in many states, but the legal requirements for
  data collection remain the same.

**Penalties:** Failure to maintain proper guest registration can result in fines of up
to **EUR 50,000**.

## Step 5: Tourist Tax (Varies by City)

Germany does not have a national tourist tax. Individual cities levy their own overnight
accommodation taxes under various names:

### Berlin - City Tax (Uebernachtungssteuer)
- **Rate:** 5% of the net accommodation price (excluding VAT).
- **Exemption:** Business travelers are exempt upon providing employer confirmation.
- **Collection:** Host must collect from guests and remit to the tax office
  (Finanzamt fuer Steuern und Finanzen).
- **Registration:** Hosts must register with the tax authority for this purpose.

### Munich - Bettensteuer (Bed Tax)
- Munich does **not** currently levy a city tourist tax (Bettensteuer). However, there
  have been ongoing political discussions about introducing one. Hosts should monitor
  developments.

### Hamburg - Kultur- und Tourismustaxe (Culture and Tourism Tax)
- **Rate:** Varies; typically a percentage of the accommodation price or a fixed
  amount per night. The rate was set around EUR 1-2 per person per night for budget
  accommodations, scaling up for higher-priced stays. Hamburg uses a progressive
  model.
- **Exemption:** Business travelers with proof.
- **Registration:** Hosts must register with the Hamburg tax office.

### Cologne - Kulturfoerderabgabe (Cultural Promotion Levy)
- **Rate:** 5% of the net accommodation price.
- **Exemption:** Business travelers exempt.
- **Collection and remittance:** By the host to the city.

### Duesseldorf - Beherbergungsabgabe
- **Rate:** Approximately 5% of the net accommodation price.
- **Exemption:** Business travelers.

### Other Cities with Tourist Tax
- **Frankfurt am Main:** Tourismusbeitrag applies.
- **Dresden:** Beherbergungssteuer at approximately EUR 1.30 per person per night
  (or percentage-based).
- **Freiburg:** Uebernachtungssteuer of approximately 5%.
- **Weimar, Bremen, Luebeck, Trier:** Various forms of overnight tax.

**Important:** Each city has its own registration, filing, and payment deadlines. Hosts
must proactively register with the local tax authority.

## Step 6: Income Tax and VAT Obligations

### Income Tax (Einkommensteuer)
- All rental income from short-term lets must be declared on the annual income tax
  return (Einkommensteuer-Erklaerung).
- Income is reported under **Einkuenfte aus Vermietung und Verpachtung** (income from
  letting and leasing) or, if the activity qualifies as commercial, under
  **Einkuenfte aus Gewerbebetrieb** (commercial income).
- **Deductible expenses** include: furnishing, cleaning, platform fees, insurance,
  repairs, depreciation (AfA), utilities, internet, and proportional mortgage interest.
- If total rental income (not just profit) is below **EUR 410 per year** as a
  supplementary income (Nebeneinkuenfte), it is tax-free under the Haerteausgleich
  provision.

### Trade Tax (Gewerbesteuer)
- If the short-term rental activity is classified as **commercial** (gewerblich) rather
  than private asset management, trade tax (Gewerbesteuer) applies in addition to
  income tax.
- The threshold for commercial classification is fact-dependent. Key indicators include:
  hotel-like services (daily cleaning, breakfast, reception), multiple properties, and
  high turnover frequency.
- **Trade tax exemption threshold:** EUR 24,500 per year for sole proprietors.
- If commercial, a **Gewerbeanmeldung** (trade registration) with the local
  Gewerbeamt is mandatory.

### VAT (Umsatzsteuer)
- Short-term accommodation is subject to a **reduced VAT rate of 7%** (not the standard
  19%). This reduced rate applies to accommodation services only; ancillary services
  (breakfast, parking, Wi-Fi if charged separately) are taxed at 19%.
- **Kleinunternehmerregelung (Small Business Exemption):** If your total annual revenue
  (Umsatz) does not exceed **EUR 22,000** (as of 2024; verify current threshold), you
  can opt for the small business exemption and not charge VAT. In this case, you cannot
  deduct input VAT.
- If VAT applies, you must register for VAT with the Finanzamt and file regular
  VAT returns (Umsatzsteuervoranmeldung), typically monthly or quarterly.

### DAC7 - Platform Reporting Obligations
- Under the **EU DAC7 Directive** (Plattformen-Steuertransparenzgesetz in German law,
  effective since January 1, 2023), digital platforms (Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo, etc.)
  are required to **report host income data** to the German Federal Central Tax Office
  (Bundeszentralamt fuer Steuern, BZSt).
- Platforms report: Host identity, tax identification number (Steuer-ID), total
  consideration received, number of transactions, property address, and account details.
- The BZSt shares this information with local tax authorities. This means tax
  authorities can cross-reference reported platform income against tax returns.
- **Action required:** Hosts must ensure their tax declarations are accurate and
  complete, as underreporting will be detected.

## Step 7: Safety Requirements

### Smoke Detectors (Rauchwarnmelder)
Smoke detectors are **mandatory in all 16 German states** as of 2024. Each state has
enacted its own smoke detector obligation through its building code
(Landesbauordnung, LBO).

**Minimum requirements (all states):**
- Smoke detectors must be installed in **all bedrooms, children's rooms, and hallways**
  that serve as escape routes.
- Some states (e.g., Berlin, Brandenburg, Thuringia) also require smoke detectors in
  **living rooms**.
- Detectors must comply with **DIN EN 14604** standard.
- The **property owner** is responsible for installation. The **occupant** (or in the
  case of vacation rentals, the host/operator) is responsible for maintenance and
  testing.

### Fire Safety
- Properties in multi-family buildings must comply with fire safety regulations per the
  relevant Landesbauordnung.
- Escape routes must be clearly marked and unobstructed.
- Many cities require a fire extinguisher to be present (varies by local regulation and
  building type).
- Emergency information, including emergency phone numbers (112 for fire/medical, 110
  for police), should be displayed prominently.

### Carbon Monoxide Detectors
- Not yet mandatory by federal law, but **strongly recommended**, especially in
  properties with gas heating, fireplaces, or wood-burning stoves.

### Electrical Safety
- All electrical installations must comply with VDE standards.
- Regular inspection of electrical systems is recommended.

### Drinking Water / Legionella
- Properties connected to a centralized hot water system with a storage volume above
  400 liters are subject to **mandatory Legionella testing** under the
  Trinkwasserverordnung (Drinking Water Ordinance). This is typically relevant for
  larger buildings.

## Step 8: Insurance Requirements

### Liability Insurance (Haftpflichtversicherung)
- While not always legally mandated specifically for vacation rentals in every state,
  **liability insurance is strongly recommended and practically essential**.
- Standard private liability insurance (Privathaftpflichtversicherung) typically
  **does not cover** damages arising from commercial short-term rental activity.
- A **Haus- und Grundbesitzerhaftpflichtversicherung** (property owner's liability
  insurance) or a specific **Vermieter-Haftpflichtversicherung** (landlord liability
  insurance) should be obtained.
- Some platforms (like Airbnb) offer host protection insurance, but this should
  **not be relied upon as a substitute** for your own policy.

### Building Insurance (Wohngebaeudeversicherung)
- Standard building insurance policies may exclude or limit coverage for properties
  used as vacation rentals. **Notify your insurer** of the intended use to avoid
  policy invalidation.
- Ensure coverage includes: fire, storm, water damage, and ideally vandalism by
  guests.

### Contents Insurance (Hausratversicherung)
- Standard contents insurance typically does not cover damage caused by paying guests.
- Consider a policy specifically designed for holiday rental furnishings, or add a
  vacation rental endorsement.

### Recommended Additional Coverage
- **Legal protection insurance** (Rechtsschutzversicherung) covering disputes with
  guests, neighbors, or authorities.
- **Loss of rental income insurance** (Mietausfallversicherung).

## Step 9: Building and Homeowners' Association Rules

### Building Regulations (Baurecht)
- The property must have a valid **occupancy permit** (Nutzungsgenehmigung) that is
  compatible with short-term rental use. A property permitted for residential use
  (Wohnnutzung) may not automatically be permitted for commercial accommodation.
- In some jurisdictions, a **change of use permit** (Nutzungsaenderungsgenehmigung)
  may be required if the activity is classified as commercial accommodation
  (Beherbergungsbetrieb) rather than residential use.
- **Building Use Zones:** In areas zoned as "Pure Residential" (Reines Wohngebiet,
  WR) under the BauNVO, vacation rental may be prohibited entirely. In "General
  Residential" (Allgemeines Wohngebiet, WA) or "Mixed Use" (Mischgebiet, MI) zones,
  it is generally permissible.

### Homeowners' Association (Wohnungseigentuemergemeinschaft, WEG)
- If the property is in a condominium (Eigentumswohnung), the **declaration of division
  (Teilungserklaerung)** and **community rules (Gemeinschaftsordnung)** may restrict
  or prohibit short-term rental.
- Under the **reformed WEG law (WEMoG, effective December 1, 2020)**, owners'
  associations can adopt resolutions to restrict short-term letting if it causes
  disturbances.
- Court decisions (e.g., BGH rulings) have established that a general clause allowing
  "residential use" in the Teilungserklaerung does **not** automatically prohibit
  short-term vacation rental, but the community may take action if the rental causes
  demonstrable harm to other owners.

### Tenant Restrictions
- If you are a **tenant** seeking to sublet short-term, you need **explicit written
  consent** from your landlord (Vermieter). The landlord may refuse consent if there
  is a legitimate reason (e.g., overcrowding, property damage risk).
- Subletting without consent can be grounds for **termination of the lease**
  (fristlose Kuendigung).

## Step 10: Platform and Advertising Requirements

### Registration Number Display
In cities with Zweckentfremdungsverbote, the registration number (Wohnraumschutznummer
or equivalent) must be displayed:
- In **every online listing** (Airbnb, Booking.com, Vrbo, etc.).
- On **any website or printed advertisement**.
- Failure to display the number is an **independent violation** subject to fines.

### Platform Cooperation with Authorities
- Major platforms (Airbnb, Booking.com) have signed agreements with several German
  cities to share data and enforce registration requirements.
- Platforms may **deactivate listings** that do not include a valid registration number
  in cities where this is required.
- Under DAC7, platforms must report host data to tax authorities regardless of
  voluntary cooperation agreements.

### Advertising Standards
- Listings must not be misleading regarding the property type, size, amenities, or
  location.
- Maximum occupancy stated in listings must comply with local regulations.
- In some cities, the registration number format is specified and must be reproduced
  exactly.

## Step 11: Sanctions and Fines

Fines for non-compliance vary by jurisdiction but can be severe:

| City / State | Maximum Fine | Key Violations |
|---|---|---|
| Berlin | EUR 500,000 | Operating without registration number, exceeding 90-day limit, renting secondary residence without permit |
| Munich | EUR 500,000 | Operating without permit, exceeding 8-week limit |
| Hamburg | EUR 500,000 | Operating without permit or registration |
| Cologne | EUR 500,000 | Operating without permit under NRW law |
| Duesseldorf | EUR 500,000 | Operating without permit under NRW law |
| Stuttgart | EUR 100,000 | Operating without permit under BW law |
| Frankfurt | EUR 500,000 | Operating without permit under Hessian law |

**Additional consequences of non-compliance:**
- **Back taxes:** Unpaid tourist tax, income tax, and VAT can be assessed retroactively
  with interest and late-payment penalties.
- **Criminal liability:** In extreme cases of tax evasion, criminal prosecution is
  possible.
- **Platform delisting:** Platforms may remove non-compliant listings.
- **Injunctions:** Authorities can issue cease-and-desist orders (Untersagungsverfuegung)
  requiring immediate cessation of the rental activity.

## Step 12: Recent Regulatory Developments

### Key Developments (2023-2025)
- **DAC7 Implementation (2023):** The EU directive on platform reporting became
  effective in Germany on January 1, 2023. Platforms began reporting host data to the
  BZSt for the 2023 tax year (first reports due January 2024). This has significantly
  increased tax transparency and enforcement.
- **Berlin Enforcement Intensification:** Berlin has continued to increase enforcement
  resources, including dedicated investigation units and automated platform monitoring.
  Multiple high-profile fines have been issued.
- **Munich Tightening:** Munich has maintained strict enforcement and has reduced the
  threshold for requiring permits. The city actively monitors platform listings.
- **Hamburg Registration Number System:** Hamburg has fully implemented its digital
  registration number system, aligning with Berlin's approach.
- **NRW Cities (Cologne, Duesseldorf):** North Rhine-Westphalia cities have expanded
  enforcement under the Wohnraumstaerkungsgesetz, with increasing numbers of fines
  issued.
- **EU Short-Term Rental Regulation:** The EU adopted a regulation on short-term rental
  data collection in 2024, which will require member states to establish registration
  systems. Germany will need to align its various municipal systems with the EU
  framework. Full implementation is expected by 2026-2027.
- **Digital Guest Registration:** Several states are moving toward fully digital
  Meldeschein systems, reducing paperwork for hosts.
- **Ongoing Court Cases:** Various German courts continue to refine the interpretation
  of Zweckentfremdungsverbote, particularly regarding the distinction between primary
  and secondary residence rental and the definition of "short-term."

## Summary Checklist

Before listing your property, ensure you have completed the following:

- [ ] Verified whether your city has a Zweckentfremdungsverbot in effect.
- [ ] Obtained a permit or registration number (Wohnraumschutznummer or equivalent) if
      required.
- [ ] Confirmed the property's zoning permits short-term rental use.
- [ ] Checked the Teilungserklaerung / homeowners' association rules (if applicable).
- [ ] Obtained landlord consent (if you are a tenant).
- [ ] Registered with the local tax authority for tourist tax (if applicable).
- [ ] Registered for income tax reporting of rental income.
- [ ] Assessed VAT obligations and registered if above the threshold.
- [ ] Filed a Gewerbeanmeldung (if activity is commercial).
- [ ] Installed smoke detectors in all required rooms.
- [ ] Ensured fire safety compliance (escape routes, emergency information).
- [ ] Obtained appropriate insurance (liability, building, contents).
- [ ] Prepared Meldeschein forms for guest registration.
- [ ] Displayed the registration number in all listings and advertisements.
- [ ] Set up procedures to collect and remit tourist tax from guests.
- [ ] Familiarized yourself with day-limits (e.g., 90 days in Berlin, 56 days in Munich).
